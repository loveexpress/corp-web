Which Sugar Is The Healthiest?

By Elizabeth Madison

What’s healthier - brown or white su­gar? Raw sugar or table sugar? Is honey better than sugar? I field these questions from clients on a daily basis in my private practice. This month I will share the answers with you as I continue the theme from last month’s topic, Sugar Substitutes. 

Natural sugars and sweeteners include white, brown and raw sugar, honey, agave nectar, and molasses. There is a pervasive misconception that some sugars / sweeteners are healthier than others because they are ‘less processed’, more ‘natural’, organic, or have more vitamins and minerals than other sweeteners. These are  all misconceptions. First, it is important to understand what is a sugar/sweetener. All sugars and sweeteners are made from a combination of simple sugars called glucose and fructose. Your body proces­ses these simple sugars equally and very quickly which increases blood sugar levels. The different types of sugars/sweeteners all contain a significant amount of calories. Excessive intake can lead to overweight and obesity. Second, ALL sugars are processed because they have to go through a refining process to go from their natural state to your table. Last­ly, the term organic refers to sustainable farming practi­ces, not nutritional quality; and the word ‘natural’ has no legal definition. Hence, sorry to say it, but no sugar/sweetener is healthier than another. Let’s take a closer look at your favorite sugar/sweetener to determine what it really means for your health.

White or table sugar is the most refined version of sugar. It contains 48 calories per tablespoon - measuring, not eating, tablespoon. Raw sugar is a type of sugar that has not undergone complete refining, leaving a tanned hue. It contains 45 calories per tablespoon. Brown sugar is white sugar that has been mixed with molasses, which adds calories, making its calorie content the highest of all the sugars, at 51 calories per tablespoon. Raw sugar and brown sugar do have slightly more minerals than their white counterpart, but the amount is not significant enough to deem them any “healthier” or “better” than white sugar. These sugars have no nutritional value.

It is a slightly different story with the natural sweeteners. Honey, agave nectar, molasses, and pure maple syrup all have a high calorie price tag: 52-60 calories per 1 tablespoon. They are all made from the simple sugars mentioned earlier, however some of these sweete- ners have medicinal benefits. Honey has antioxidants, and anti-bacterial and anti-inflammatory properties, which is great when fighting a cold.  As a general rule, the darker the honey, the better its antioxidant and anti-bacterial properties. Never give a child under 1 year of age honey due to the risk of botulism. Pure maple syrup also contains anti-bacterial and antioxidant properties as well (don’t confuse this with pancake syrup!). Agave nectar and molasses offer no nutritional benefits; in fact, agave nectar has a very high amount of fructose, the intake of which has been linked with insulin resistance.

Though honey and ma­ple syrup have some additional nutritional benefits, this doesn’t make them healthier. They are still high in calories, and convert quickly to glucose which can drive up blood sugar levels. Some people believe that natural sweeteners are safe for people with diabetes to consume. Though the glycemic index of some natural sweeteners is lower, there is not enough evidence to prove that they won’t raise blood sugar levels like any other sugar/sweetener. The key to using these sweeteners and all sugars is moderation. For more information about all types of sugars and sweeteners, visit  www.eatright. org, the website of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics.
