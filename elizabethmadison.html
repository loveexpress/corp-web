Sugar Substitutes

Part One

By Elizabeth Madison

The media and the internet are full of confusing and often contradictory information about sugar substitutes, like Equal, Splenda and Stevia. Since God desires us to be educated, as stated in Proverbs 18:15, An intelligent heart acquires knowledge, and the ear of the wise seeks knowledge,  I am providing this ‘Frequently Asked Questions’ guide in two parts (it IS a lot of information!). I hope this helps you sort through all the noise and make informed decisions.

What is the difference between artificial and natural sweeteners? Generally, artificial sweeteners are man-made and natural sweeteners are derived from organic (from the earth) compounds. However, these terms are not absolute. For example, some manufacturers label their sweeteners as “natural” even though they are highly processed and refined, as in the case with Stevia; whereas others label their sweeteners as “artificial” even though they are derived from naturally occurring substances like amino acids, which are the building blocks in Equal.

What is the difference between ‘natural’ sweeteners and regular sugar? ‘Regular’ or ‘table’ sugar is extracted from sugarcane and sugar beets; ‘natural’ sweeteners are extracted from other sources. For example, Truvia and Stevia come from the stevia plant, and agave nectar from the agave plant. 

Why use artificial or natural sweeteners? High intake of calories, including added sugars, has been linked to many health concerns, e.g. overweight and obesity, type 2 diabetes, inflammation and cardiovascular diseases. Sugar substitutes may decrease the total number of calories found in your favorite foods, which may help with weight management.  Another benefit is since sugar substitutes are not made from carbohydrates, they won’t spike blood glucose levels, a critical goal in diabetes management. However, using sugar substitutes is only effective if you don’t make up the calories by overeating other foods.

Is calorie-free really calorie-free? Sugar substitutes are NOT calorie-free! The United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regulations state that products that are less than 5 calories per serving may be labeled as ‘calorie-free’. In other words, if consumed in large amounts, the calories quickly add up. This also goes for products labeled ‘sugar-free’. Products may be labeled ‘sugar-free’ or ‘made without added sugars’ if the product contains less than 0.5 grams of sugars per serving. So what does that mean for your favorite sugar substitute? Equal and Splenda have 4 calories per packet; Sweet n’ Low, Stevia and Truvia have slightly less than 4 calories per packet.

Are they safe? Are there side effects?  This is the #1 question I am asked.  Before a manufacturer can bring a food product to market, it must conduct scientific testing and research as directed by the FDA to prove the safety of the product for the general population. Food products that pass this rigorous process achieve a pre-market approval status, Generally Regarded As Safe (GRAS), and are then able to sell their product on the market to consumers. Unfortunately, sometimes, some side effects are not identified until either further research has been conducted post-market or after thousands of people have used the product and reported adverse reactions. This has been the case with some of the sugar substitutes. I pray that God continue to keep you all in good health!
