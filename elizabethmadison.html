How to Pick the Right 'Diet'

By Elizabeth Madison

It’s the New Year, and you may be super motivated to lose weight. You have undoubtedly been bombar­ded with advertisements for the latest diets - you know, the ones that promise to help you lose 10 pounds in a week, or slim down without any effort. Before you turn over your hard earned dollars to buy that new book or program, read on to learn the pros and cons about some of the most popular programs and how to choose a healthy and safe food plan, and NOT a ‘diet’.

A lot of the diets on the market are based on pseudoscience - theories and me­thods that are not based on scientific facts. They blend a little legitimate science with a lot of fiction to make the theories appear logical. People may lose weight on these diets, but it is mostly due to the elimination of major food groups, like carbohydrates. This leads to a reduction in calories, which is the real reason for the weight loss.  Here are a few that I feel fall into this category:

The Blood Type Diet. This diet states that you should eat according to your blood type for maximum energy and health. The author proposes that blood types were developed at different times during human evolution, and thus we ate differently during these periods. For example, Blood Group O was the first blood type of our ancestors who were hunters-gatherers around 50,000 B.C. Because they subsisted on a high animal protein diet, people who have this blood type should do the same, and not eat any dairy or wheat or grain products. There is no valid science behind this theory.  Your blood type has nothing to do with what you eat.

The Paleo Diet. Also known as the Caveman diet, the underlying belief is that if we ate like our ancestors did thousands of years ago, we would be healthier, lose weight and curb disease. The general diet focuses on meat and fish, as well as shellfish, poultry, eggs, veggies, root, fruits and berries, while excluding dairy, legumes, su­gar and salt. In other words, if the cavemen didn’t eat it, you shouldn’t either. This may sound like a great diet as it limits salt and sugar intake, while promoting fruit and vegetable intake, but eli­minating whole grains, le­gu­mes and dairy is risky as they are great sources of fi­ber, vitamins and minerals. Moreover, high protein diets can be high in cholesterol.

Acid-Alkaline Diet. This principle behind this diet is that certain foods promote acid more than others, which would in turn can lead to decreased bone health, heart health, and higher risk of dia­betes and cancer. In actuality, the body is very efficient at maintaining a constant pH regardless of what we eat. Again, there is no evidenced based science supporting this approach.

The Fast Diet. This diet instructs you to eat normally 5 times a week and fast the other 2 days - you are limited to 500 calories (female) or 600 calories (male). This is dangerous because your bo­dy will interpret the extreme decrease in calories as starvation. It will deplete your ve­ry necessary glucose stores and begin to breakdown lean body mass. Of course you lose weight, but it is not sustainable nor is it a healthy approach to weight and health management.

I can continue because there are so many unsound diets out there, but I hope you get the point. Diets are not ideal because they are short-term – they don’t last long because you can’t stick to it. A food plan is a structure for eating each day that can last a lifetime. So how do you choose a safe and healthy food plan?

First, no plan should provide less than approximately 1200 calories (unless your 4’11). Anything below that will launch you into starvation/ deprivation mode. Try to eat less than this for a few days and you’ll eat the paint off the wall because you’re so hungry. Second, a healthy food plan will include all the food groups - including carbohydrates. You need the nutrients from these foods to support good health. The food plan will emphasize portion control - there is no way around it, you have to eat less to weigh less. Most importantly, it must feature flexibility and adaptability to one’s lifestyle. You want a food plan you can see yourself maintaining a year or two from now, not just for the next few weeks. A healthy food plan will foster gradual lifestyle changes that you’ll feel good about, which will increase your ability to sustain them.

Here are some of my favo­rite food plans that meet these criteria:

Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) Diet. Based on studies sponsored by the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute (NHLBI), it emphasizes vegetables, fruits and fat-free or low-fat dairy, and includes whole grains, fish, poultry, legu­mes, and vegetable oils, while limiting sodium, added sugars, and red meats. It has been proven to lower high blood pressure, improve lipid profile, and reduce risk of developing cardiovascular disea- ses.

Therapeutic Lifestyle Changes (TLC) Diet. TLC was developed by the Natio­nal Institutes of Health (NIH) and endorsed by the American Heart Association to help decrease cholesterol. It includes diet, physical activity, weight loss, and smoking cessation. This food plan aims to limit the amount of saturated fat you eat by reducing (not eliminating) the amount of meat and whole dairy products consumed.

Mayo Clinic Diet. This diet incorporates physical activity, portion control, and menu planning, while encou­raging unlimited amounts of vegetables and fruit. It provides the tools to lose weight gradually and permanantly without eliminating any food group! The focus is on low-calorie, nutrient dense foods that include whole-grains, lean protein, fish, low-fat dairy and heart-healthy unsaturated fats.

You can see the healthy thread running through these food plans. Now, not all diets are right for everyone. I encourage you to check these out and make the right choice for you. If you have a medi­cal condition or specific concern, consult with a Registered Die­titian to assist you in developing a plan just for you.
