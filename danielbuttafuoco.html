What is Revival and Why
Do We Need It?

by Daniel P. Buttafuoco Esq.

The word “revival” is not to be found in either the NIV or the KJV translations of the Bible. The closest English word to revival in the Bible is the word “revive” from which the word “revival” is derived and it is found in Psalm 85:6. That Psalm is a “communal prayer for the renewal of God’s mercies to his people at a time when they are once more suffering from distress.” It is a prayer of restoration, in part, as follows:

Restore us again, O God our Savior, and put away your displeasure toward us.
Will you be angry with us forever?
Will you prolong your anger through all generations?
Will you not revive us again, that your people may rejoice in you?(Psalms 85:4-6; NIV, emphasis mine)

According to these verses, revival is for God”s people (i.e. the Church). While revival often has the connotation of camp meetings and evangelical success in winning new converts, in reality, revival is defined as “the act or process of bringing...back to life.” It is not creating new life out of thin air, nor is it bringing to life inanimate matter since that was never alive to begin with. Rather, revival connotes the idea of returning to a state of life what once was alive but which since has died.

When looking at the pathetic state of some churches and the miserable condition of the lives of many so called believers it is easy to see why revival is needed. All recent statistics indicate that the lifestyle differences between people claiming to be followers of Christ (“Christians”) and non-believers is virtually non-existent. Thus, when it comes to divorce, abortion, criminality, pre-marital and extra-marital sex, lying, cheating and stealing, there is little observable difference between “the world” and “the church.” Is it any wonder that our testimony as believers is bankrupt in the eyes of society?

My facsimile edition of Websters 1828 American Dictionary of the English Language defines revival as “renewed and more active attention to religion [i.e. Christianity]; an awakening of men [and women] to their spiritual concerns.” In light of the statistically demonstrated lack of character in the Church, mentioned above, is there any doubt that we need revival now more than ever? We desperately need more active attention to the matters of our faith and an awakening to spiritual concerns for the sake of Christ who purchased us for himself at great cost. The clear teachings of the Bible in which we are called to serve our God for his good purpose and pleasure have been replaced by a self-centered “gospel” message, taught by many popular preachers of “what can God do for me lately.” It is a terrible tragedy of modern American Christianity that the Christ centered message of the cross has been reduced to a man-centered appeal to the basest desires of human nature, in the name of “faith.” We need a revival and we need it now.

“The church is in need of perennial revival because of recurrent spiritual decline.” Just as the Israelites in the Book of Judges went from captivity, to miraculous deliverance, to praising God, to apostasy and idolatry and then back to captivity, only to have the cycle repeated shamelessly over and over, the Church suffers the same fate in a similar cyclical fa­shion. It is the problem of human nature (“carnal nature” in the NIV; the “flesh” in the KJV) which, when not suppressed, causes believers to gradually grow cold to the rigorous demands of true Christian living and to slow­ly but surely forsake the commands of Christ. This is sometimes described as “losing one’s first love [for Christ].” Love is a verb, however; an action word.  Jesus said “if you love me, keep my commandments (John 14:15).” Yes, Christ’s commandments are often difficult but we are not without the help of the Holy Spirit (“helper”) to assist us daily in this regard. We are commanded in scripture to “find out what pleases the Lord (Eph. 5:10)“ and, of course, to do it!

I often teach my Bible students that it is easy to become a Christian but hard to become a disciple. By that I mean that Christ has made it easy for us to obtain forgiveness of our sins (it’s all him!) but it is hard to emulate the self-sacrifice of Christ (and his earliest followers, for that matter). Self-indulgent fashionable “gos­pels” that “tickle the ears” of the public to “name it and claim it” do not help in this regard. Perhaps it is exactly because we teach that Christ’s forgiveness is so easy and cheap that believers today feel they can neglect prayer and spiritual disciplines and just “run to the altar” for a quick and easy repentance. That is dangerous living, my friends! Christ calls us to holiness and, the longer you have known him and had his fellowship, the higher the standard. If you are a believer you will manifest the spiritual fruits listed in Galations 5:22: “love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control.” Against such character traits there is no need for law!

In a genuine revival, Christ and not the human self, not the celebrity prea­chers and not anything else has the pre-eminence. When we focus on ourselves we inevitably return to apostasy. When we focus on Christ, we are “on fire” and revival erupts. Why else does the Church always prosper amidst the fires of severe persecution? Why did the early Church explode in the face of severe recriminations including torture and martyrdom? The answer is simply because they had no choice but to shift their fo­cus from themselves onto Christ who is worth more than everything this world has to offer.

We here in the spoiled rotten self-centered American Church need revival more now than at anytime in history. It’s not about us, it’s about him. Revive us, O Lord!

The word “revival” derives from the Latin word “revivere” meaning “to live again.” In the context used here it is a sovereign work of God which brings new life to the Church.
