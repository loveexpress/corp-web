Pre-Diabetes:
Knowledge is Power!

By Elizabeth Madison

For National Diabetes Month, I always focus on pre-diabetes. Why? Because though 29.1 million Americans had diabetes in 2012, 86 million had pre-diabetes. Of those, 77 million don’t even know it. Up to 30% of those individuals with pre-diabetes will develop Type 2 diabetes within five years. Lack of awareness and denial allows this disease to prog-ress.  To help you stay on top of your health, here is a quick Q&A.

What is diabetes/pre-diabetes? Diabetes is a condition that results in too much sugar (glucose) in the blood. This can be caused by the body making little to no insulin (a hormone that helps regulate blood sugar), or the body not being able to use sugar properly. Pre-diabetes results when blood sugar levels are high, but not as high as in diabetes. It is also known as borderline diabetes. How do I know if I have pre-diabetes? The hemoglobin A1c test measures your average blood glucose for the previous 2-3 months. An A1c of 5.7 – 6.4% will indicate pre-diabetes. This should be tested no more than every 3 months. All insurance plans will pay for this test, so get it done asap! Am I at risk for pre-diabetes? Any of the following risk factors puts you at risk: overweight/obese, 45 years of age or older, being African-American, Native-American, Asian-American, Pacific Islander, or Alaskan native; high blood pressure, low levels of ‘good’ HDL cholesterol, high levels of triglycerides, a history of gestational diabetes or polycystic ovary syndrome; family history of diabetes; a sedentary lifestyle. You may not be able to control some of these risk factors, but you can control others, and this is where you can focus your efforts.

How can I prevent or slow the progression of pre-diabetes?  Nutrition and exercise are the first line of defense for managing pre-diabetes. Here are 5 tips to keep in mind:

1) Lose weight. If you are currently overweight, just losing 5-10% of your body weight can reduce your risk of diabetes.

2) Eat healthy. Choose foods low in calories, fat, and added sugars. Instead, eat foods that are high in fiber, focusing on fresh fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Make ½ your plate non-starchy veggies for breakfast, lunch, dinner.

3) Get moving. Aim for at least 30-60 minutes of moderate physical activity most days of the week. Exercise improves blood glucose control.

4) Get tested. The only way to know if have pre-diabetes is to get tested. Don’t delay! 5) Commit and stay in prayer. Changing habits can be challenging and overwhelming. Being in denial or procrastinating won’t make pre-diabetes go away. Before you make any change, ask God to help you get started and stay committed. You can do all things through Christ who strengthens you. For more information on preventing and managing diabetes and pre-diabetes, visit www. niddk.gov, or www.diabetes.org.
