Protect Your Bones!

By Elizabeth Madison

Are your bones in good health? Do you know how to take good care of them? If you’re not sure, this is a great time to learn about bone health, as May is Osteoporosis Prevention Month. Osteoporosis is a condition that thins and wea­kens bones, increasing risk of fractures. It is a silent disease, so you may not even realize you have it until you have a slip or fall. However, there are steps you can take to keep your bones in good health and prevent a life-altering accident.

First, know your risk status. Though anyone can get osteoporosis, there are a number of factors that may increase your risk: 1) older age - it is estimated that 50% of all women and 25% of men over 50 will fracture a bone due to osteoporosis. 2) Being female, especially after menopause. 3) Body size – petite, thin women are at greater risk. 3) Family history of osteoporosis. 4) Taking medications like steroids or some anti-depressants. 5) Certain pre-existing conditions and diseases such as eating disorders, rheumatoid arthritis, gastrointestinal disorders, bariatric surgeries and thyroid problems. 6) Ethnicity. It is a common misconception that osteoporosis affects only Caucasian and Asian women. In fact, osteoporosis rates are similar in both Caucasian women and Hispanic women. African-Americans tend to have a higher bone mineral density than Caucasians. However, they are still at risk because as many as 75% of all African-Americans are lactose sensitive or intolerant (or believe they are). This decreases optimal calcium and Vitamin D intake.

Though some of these risk factors are beyond your control, there are other risk factors that you can manage. Excessive intake of so­dium, caffeine and alcohol, and an inadequate diet all contribute to poor bone health. Nutritional status plays an important role in preventing osteoporosis. Here are some actions you can take to decrease your risk of osteoporosis and make you feel better overall.

Eat a well-balanced diet.  The major nutrients for good bone health are calcium and vitamin D. Other essential nutrients include magnesium, potassium, and Vitamins C and K. Calcium keeps your bones strong, supporting movement, whereas Vitamin D helps your body absorb calcium—they work toge­ther. Adults under age 50 need 1000mgs (milligrams) of calcium and 400-800 I.U. (international units) per day, 1200mgs of calcium and 800-1000 IU for over 50.

How to get enough calcium and Vitamin D? Calcium can be found in dairy products (milk, cheese, yogurts), broccoli, fortified cereals, sardines, tofu and soy products, salmon, black eyed peas, almond, okra, and dark leafy greens (collard greens, turnip greens, kale, mustard greens). If you choose to take a calcium supplement, make sure you are not taking doses greater than 500mg at a time to ensure maximum absorption.  Some calcium supplements need to be taken with food, others not, so read the label carefully.

Vitamin D is found in three sources: sunlight, food, and supplements. Food sour­ces include fish (salmon, cod, mackerel, tuna, and herring), liver and egg yolk. Some foods are fortified with Vitamin D, such as milk, some yogurts, orange juice, soy beverages, and breakfast cereals.   Ask your doctor to determine if you have a Vitamin D deficiency –and don’t wait! You may need supplements immediately to bring your Vitamin D levels into a healthy range. However, before you choose a supplement, speak to a Registered Dietitian to ensure you are taking the right one based on your current supplement intake and medications. 

Staying active plays an important role in preventing osteoporosis. Certain forms of exercise can build bone density and slow bone loss. The National Osteoporosis Foundation recommends at least 30 minutes of weight-bearing exercises (dancing, walking, gardening, low-impact aerobics, stair climbing) three to four days a week, and strengthening exercises (free weights, weight machines, elastic exercise bands) two to three times a week. Balance and posture exercises such as tai chi or yoga may also help decrease the risk of falls and broken bones.
Today is a great day to begin improving your bone health. But before you make any changes to your lifestyle, commit to God first, trust in Him, and he will make your wellness journey possible (Psalms 37:5). It is God’s will for you to be in optimal health.
