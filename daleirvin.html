The Fulton Street Prayer And
The Great Awakening
1857-1858 New York City and the Churches
150th Anniversary of the Fulton Street Prayer

by Rev. Dr. Dale T. Irvin

New York City was the epicenter of the most important re­ligious revivals of the 19th century. The Businessmen’s Revival, as it is often cal­led, began on Sep­t. 23, 1857 in North Dutch Re­formed Church on the corner of Ful­ton and William Streets in the lower Man­hattan. It began with a mid-day prayer service at­tended by businessmen working in the financial district and grew to a citywide pheno­menon. From New York the revival quickly spread to other cities as well, eventually helping to redefine the religious landscape of the nation.

Jeremiah C. Lanphi­er was by all accounts an un­likely candidate to start a national revival. A businessman himself, he had been converted some years before in the Broadway Tabernacle that had been founded by Charles Finney (himself one of the great re­vivalists of US history). Lanphier had begun working as a lay outreach minister for the Fulton Street Re­formed Church. The na­tion’s cities were rapidly growing in the 1850s and business was booming. But church membership had be­gun to dwindle. Lanphier noted that many of the businessmen who came into the lower Manhattan neighborhood every day had only re­cently arrived in the city, and were often without a church home.

Thus he decided to an­nounce a mid-day prayer service at the Fulton Street Church. The first day he opened up the third floor and sat for the first 30 minutes alone. Around 12:30 one, then two, then several more businessmen made their way up the stairs, until a total of six had gathered for prayer. The following week he opened the church again and twenty came in. The third week forty attended. Lanphier began holding daily services from 12 to 1pm for prayer, moving the meeting to the second floor where a larger room could accommodate the rapidly growing numbers.

Word of the revival spread quickly. The financial collapse of the markets and a run on the banks that October that resulted in the Panic of 1857 helped fuel the turn to prayer. In Fe­bruary the YMCA, which had only arrived in New York City several years be­fore, began organizing similar mid-day prayer services for businessmen in other churches, including the John Street Methodist Epis­copal Church. The Metho­dists soon had a band known as the Flying Ar­til­le­ry of Heaven visiting their churches throughout the city to spread the re­vi­val.

In Brooklyn, Ply­mouth Congregational Church be­gan to overflow with the businessmen coming in each day for prayer. Henry Ward Beecher, the well-known pastor of the church, was joined for a time by his equally famous sister, Har­riet Beecher Stowe, in leading these services in 1858. The YMCA rented Bur­ton’s Theater in lower Manhattan for several weeks of services. News­papers such as the Herald and the Tribune be­gan running stories on the revival during the spring of 1858. Horace Greeley, editor of the Tribune, took a particular interest in the course that events were taking.

Among the most im­portant long term effects of the Revival were the in­di­vi­duals who were affected by it. Arthur Tappan Pier­son, one of the great names in 19th century missions and a successful (if sometimes controversial) urban pastor, was a student at Union Theo­lo­gi­cal Se­mi­nary (then located in lower Man­hat­tan) and was teaching a Bi­ble class at Thir­teenth Street Pres­by­terian Church during the Revival. His life was de­finitely shaped by its im­pact. Dwight L. Moody was only 21 and living in Chi­ca­go during the Revival of 1857-1858, but no one learned more than he the lessons of bringing religion to the downtown business district and involving successful business leaders at every stage.

Eventually the fires of the Businessmen’s Revival in New York died down, but when it was over the city’s religious landscape was dramatically changed. One of the most obvious results was the dramatic rise in church membership in all denominations. Es­ti­mates run as high as 50,000 new members joining chur­ches in the city as a result of the awakening. Second, the re­vival of 1857-1858 was in large part lay-led, and geared toward the business community, not only in NYC but through­out the nation. It was the first such revival to be so in US church history, the first such effort to take spirituality into the business world in such an ex­plicit manner.

For more information, Kethryn Teresa Long, The Revival of 1857-58: Inter­preting an American Re­li­gious Awakening. NY: Ox­ford University Press, 1998.

Reprinted from Lea­der­ship NY, 2006 Spring & Summer edition. Dale T. Irvin, Act­ing Pre­si­dent and Pro­fessor of World Chris­tianity, New York Theo­lo­gical Semi­nary.
