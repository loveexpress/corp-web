The 21st Century Church

by Edward Schefter
Senior Pastor New York City Church

The fastest growing forms of communication today – Twitter, MySpace, Facebook, and Wordpress – all have one thing in common: to develop an online social community that connects people from all walks of life. This is the reality of today’s generation, made possible through the advances of technology and the Internet. And while family and friends have changed the way they communicate and stay connected in the 21st Century, so too should churches.

The fact that membership has exponentially increased at churches with an online presence than simply a physical location alone is astounding. Take The Potter’s House in Dallas, Texas for example, the 30,000 plus member church not only has Facebook and MySpace accounts along with a Website with updated blogs and an RSS feed (a function that allows people to be informed when something new has been posted), but services are streamed online. The church also claims “Christian Today and the Dallas Morning News tag The Potter's House as one of the fastest growing churches in the nation.” Not too bad for a church that began with 50 families in 1996.

Likewise, at New York City Church (which began about a year ago and now resides in the Times Square Arts Center), we believe it is essential to our growth and ability in reaching the people of this city, state, region, and beyond by developing an online presence along with having a physical location. Our core values are simple: to be real, relevant and relational. And if we are to achieve these values and instill them to our members, we know one thing is for certain: we need to reach people where they are just as Jesus reached the people of his day where they were. So if most, especially the younger generations, are online, we too need to do the same.

We are not the Church of yesterday, but today. And since “Jesus Christ is the same yesterday, today and forever” (Hebrews 13:8), we can be sure that He calls us to affect the culture of our times. This means we must use whatever communication vehicle currently available to us in the 21st Century, whether blogging, Website presence and streaming video, Twitter updates, Facebook or MySpace accounts, etc. By doing so, we are not only sharing the gospel in ways that we could never have imagined, but to relate with one another in the busyness of life and heal the relational fragmentation in our communities. These modern day tools of communication can help the Church reach people who are hungry for the God of today, yet are apprehensive of what they may find by merely walking into a house of worship.

Remember, this is the finest hour for the Church. Now is the time to reach this generation for such a time as this, a time when the Church must rise to the call of God to develop and leverage the technological tools God has placed in our hands.
