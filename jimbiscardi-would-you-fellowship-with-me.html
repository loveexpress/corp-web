Would You Fellowship With Me?

by Jim Biscardi Jr.

Jesus said, “Abide in Me, and I in you.  As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself, except it abide in the vine; no more can you except you abide in Me. I am the vine, you are the branches.  He that abides in Me, and I in him, the same brings forth much fruit: for without Me, you can do nothing.” John 15: 4-5.

Fellowship is one of the six important ways we stay connected to [abide in] Christ.

The others are Prayer, Studying the Word, Worship, Service, and Comforting others in their trials with the comfort God comforted us with during our own trials. 

The life of a Christian is a shared life. We are expected to help one another in both spiritual and material ways when God leads us. Acts 2:42-46 says that the early Christians devoted themselves to fellowship. They just didn’t have fellowship; they devoted themselves to it. Fellowship was one of the objectives for gathering together.

The Apostle Paul said, “You, my brothers, were called to be free. But do not use your freedom to indulge your sinful nature; rather, serve one another in love.” Galatians 5:13

One of the ways we serve one another is to help each other grow in Christ.  All of us are works in progress who are growing to emulate Christ.  In general, the church helps members grow in Christ by providing many opportunities to learn the Word and to practice the Word through service to others, and times of worship, fellowship, prayer, and comfort.

We’re familiar with most

of those opportunities, but what about fellowship?  What’s that all about? 

Fellowship is a lot like a friendship relationship in a spiritual context. It means that we befriend others, who also have a relationship with Christ, by submitting to each other in Christ (Eph. 5:21), helping one another grow spiritually (4:13-16), and sharing life – our testimony, our victories and struggles, our appointments and disappointments-our relationship with Jesus Christ. It means sharing our interests, goals, feelings, experiences, and concerns. It means being partners who work together and care for one another.

We all, at times, need someone to lean on. We need to bear one another’s burdens. Galatians 6:2. But as we fellowship, we need to always remember that these “spiritual friends” only help us draw closer to Christ. They don’t replace Him, who is the “one mediator between God and men” 1 Tim. 2:5

We fellowship when we share how the Lord is both our possessor and dispossessor (Isaiah 45: 5-8) as we surrender our life to the One who is the Blessed Controller of all things. We fellowship when we receive Christ from one another by obeying His “one another” instructions.

There are thirty “one another” scriptures in the New Testament. Here’s a sample..

Love one another. Jn 15:17; 1 Peter 1:22

Restore one another in the spirit of meekness. Gal. 6:1

Bear ye one another’s burdens. Gal. 6:2

Serve one another. Gal. 5:13

Do not envy or provoke one another. Gal. 5:25

Submit your selves one to another.  Eph. 5:21

Pray for one another. Eph. 6:18

Prefer one another. Rom. 12:10

Forbearing one another and forgiving one another. Col. 3:13

Teach and admonish one another.  Col.3:16

Comfort one another. 2 Cor. 1:4; 1Thess. 4:18

Exhort (encourage) and edify one another.  1Thess. 5:11

Greet one another. 2 Thess. 5:26

Receive one another. Rom. 15:7

Be members of one another.  Rom. 12:4

Be devoted to one another  with brotherly love. Rom. 12:10

Be tender-hearted one to another. Eph. 4:32

Lay down your lives for one another. 1John 3:16

Meet the needs of one another. 1John 3:17

In Our Daily Bread, Dave McCasland tells about a group of people who share a strong bond of faith in Christ.  One of them is a 93-year-old woman, who said that she felt she could call any one in the group even at 2 am for any type of assistance.  Whether the need is prayer, practical help, or someone to be there in time of need, these friends are unconditionally committed to each other.

We need to wonder if we’re not missing something in our Christian life if we’re not part of a “2 am group.”  Maybe we need to ask the Lord to bring us into fellowship with at least one other person with whom we can share a commitment to pray and care.

Over the years, I’ve seen that belonging to a small group of believers is a fantastic way to befriend other Christians-to pray and care for them–and to pour my life into them.  It’s also a great way for visitors to a church to get acquainted quickly with the brethren and learn about the church.

These groups can be home groups or groups that meet in church – like Sunday school classes or men’s, women’s, couples, or youth bible studies.  These groups are also good to get more personalized teaching. Generally, they are more informal than church worship services, where God’s Word is preached.  Sermons are more inspirational. Bible studies and small fellowship groups have different goals than sermons. These groups aim more at information and education rather than inspiration.

In small groups, question and answer times are considered very important ways to learn and grow in Christ. It’s also a great place for meeting and fellowshipping with other church members; and a place where people can pray more specifically for a person’s need.

In my experience, these groups are normally moderated by men and women who are appointed by the church leadership.  Some can be strictly Bible studies. Others may be strictly for fellowship. Some may be a combination of the two. In all cases, they become fun and family-building times.

Small groups are also a safe place to be because normally the church oversees and guides them through the deacons who attend (either regularly or occasionally).

Have you asked anyone, “Would you fellowship with me?”
