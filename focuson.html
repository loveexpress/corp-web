Focus On The Children:

Silent No More! Let's Talk About LGBT (continued)

by Elder Charlotte Hall

Hello Beloved: The letters LGBT are fast becoming familiar as we discussed last time. They stand for Lesbian, Gay, Bise­xual, Transgender. This subject leaves us with much to process and lingering thoughts. What do we do, how do we handle a friend, a loved one, a son or daughter that is in the throws of a gender crisis? Whether this subject has touched you perso- nally or not, it’s time to prepare in prayer and learn as much information on the subject as we can.

 1. What to do if someone in your sphere of influence comes out as gay, lesbian, bisexual or transgender? Be as supportive as you possibly can while not compromising. Every person in the LGBT community’s worst fear is that of rejection. No matter what our personal beliefs, fears or prejudices, we need to let that person know that you love them, especially when the person comes to you in their truth. That person is the same person they were before coming out of the closet. Try to remember, someone’s sexual orientation is just one part of who they are. A side note here is this: when a person chooses not to come out in truth and “preys” on others, not only are they walking in two forms of deception, my perception of them would be that of a predator. (More about those who choose to remain in hiding to be addressed in a forthcoming article.)

 2. Talk openly with the person. If you feel comfortable asking questions about their sexual orientation, do so. But we don’t have to focus solely on sexual orientation. Develop the art of listening. Concentrate on keeping the bonds of trust strong.

 3. Together seek the help of a supportive counselor. Where no counsel is the people fall: but in the multitude of counselors there is safety. Prov.11:14 Seek out a church that may be bold enough to have a ministry to this community. Pray for the person continuously. Only the Holy Spirit can show us what to do, what to say and how to minister to the person. Although this may be difficult at the time, but we must remember the only person we can change is ourselves. The rest is up to God, the person and the Holy Spi­rit.

 3. When a person comes out, they often question their place in society. They wonder if and where they will fit in. Will they still have a fami­ly? How will their church or faith community accept them? Will their friends accept or reject them. Here in lies the test. Will we as Christians add to their feelings of isolation? Or will we seek God’s grace and wisdom and love the person, not the sin? We can’t be silent or wait to determine our response. Our decision can’t be made in a moment of time, but well before an encounter.
