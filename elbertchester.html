Church Tax Compliance

by Elbert T. Chester

I have seen so much in 22 years what I cannot tell or write about, consequently too much information in the wrong hands can be dangerous, however, what I can do is express to you to invaluable experience of information that will not only benefit the Leadership, but the management and the membership. The IRS has prepared guidelines for Ministers to follow and it is by this guide, agents are instructed and guided that addresses the concerns of Ministers and their tax dealings. The number of minister’s tax returns being filed incorrectly is in excess of 75% on a nationwide level.

Ministers are not tax or financial experts; they are called by God and anointed to preach the word of God regardless of size, venue or income. Apparently, the tax preparers that prepare these returns are not experts either. The tax laws are so complicated with new legislation being introduced every year with new tax codes it becomes a full time occupation just to keep up with Congress. Pastors and leaders need qualified persons to address their needs in order to maximize their benefits legally.

One of the first topics that we will address is whether the Minister/Pastor is an employee or an independent contractor of the church. This distinction must be made on common law grounds. The application is based on the employer control over the manner in which the work is performed, either actual or the right to it, this is the basic test. In other words actual control need not be exercised by the employer provided the employer has the right to control the employee when it is appropriate and necessary.

The control test must be applied only after taking into account the nature of the work to be performed. When the worker is a professional, such as a minister, the extent of control necessary for the professional to qualify as an employee is less than that necessary for a nonprofessional worker performing routine and mechanical duties. James v.Commissioner, 25 T.C. 1296 (1956). The absence of the need to control the manner in which the minister conducts his or her duties should not be confused with the absence of the right to control. The right of control contemplated by the common law as an incident of employment requires only such supervision as the nature of the work requires. McGuire v. United States, 349 F.2d 644, 646 (9th Cir. 1965).
