Men:Time to Focus on Your Health!

By Elizabeth Madison

85% of my clients are women. Why so few men? Some key reasons. First, women are better at prioritizing their health. When I reach out to men to talk about their health, I get the following responses: they're too busy', have other things to focus on', or generally feel that they are 'not doing too badly'. When men do come to see me in my private practice, it is usually because they just had a heart attack or other serious cardiovascular event, or were recently diagnosed with diabetes or super high cholesterol. Another reason is ego. Men feel they don't need support, they can 'do this on their own'. But the reality is this: men need to make their health a priority and they need support to make the necessary changes. Men have higher rates of cardiovascular disease and associated mortality than women. African-American and Hispa­nic men have higher rates with mortality and illness than other groups. What is really amazing is that of the top 10 leading causes of deaths in men, 6 are preventable by diet and lifestyle modification. Heart disease tops the list, closely followed by cancer. Other preventable leading causes of death include stroke, diabetes, and kidney disease. So for this month, in honor of Father's Day and Men's Health Month, here are some tips for getting all you male readers started on your road to wellness.

Get good rest. This is such a basic need, but so few men get enough sleep. And the long-term effects are devastating. Inadequate sleep is linked to several chronic conditions, including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, obesity, and depression. Lack of sleep is also responsible for unintentional injuries due to motor vehicle and machinery-related accidents, the leading cause of death in males under 44 years old. Strive for 7-8 hours a night so your body can do all its repair work and re­charge itself. You will wake up more refreshed and energized. You'll also need less caffeine and sugary foods to keep you going throughout the day.

Toss the tobacco. It is never too late to quit. Quitting sooner not only improves overall health, but also reduces your risk of heart disease, cancer, lung disease, and other smoking-related illnesses. Kicking the habit is really hard, so don't do it solo. There are lots of free resources to help, like www. smokefree.org.

Also, see a dietitian to support you through the pro­cess. Why? Because nicotine is known to increase metabolism and suppress appetite. Once you stop smoking your metabolism decreases and your appetite is enhanced. A dietitian can help you develop a healthy eating plan to avoid the weight gain.

Get going. You must move. Period. And you must do it almost daily. Your heart needs it as well as your lungs, muscles and even your brain. Exercise can help normalize both blood pressure and blood sugar levels, as well as lower weight. My clients experience significant improvement in their weight, and heart health once they start exercising. And this can happen in as little as 2 weeks! It is recommended that adults get at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week. What type of activity? Any exercise that you like doing. What counts as moderate-intensity? If you can carry a tune easily while working out, you’re not working hard enough. So get moving.

Eat healthy. There's no way around it, you are what you eat. You know that eating foods high in salt, sugar, and saturated fat can result in poor health. Diets high in salt may contribute to high blood pressure, and eventually heart disease or even stroke. High sugar diets may increase both weight and insulin resistance, putting you at risk for a compromised immune system and diabetes. Too much saturated fat, found mostly in animal protein, can clog arteries. This restricts both blood and nutrient flow increasing your risk of cardiovascular disease.

It can be challenging to make the necessary chan­ges for good health. Your approach will determine your success: making dramatic and fast changes just leads to feelings of deprivation, and eventual self-sabotage. Instead, make small changes gradually. Each week or so, make a goal to change a habit. For example, cut your soda intake in half, eat a healthy breakfast 4x a week, don't use table salt, exercise for 30 minutes, 3x a week. Once you feel comfortable with the change, add another one. I have found this approach very successful with my clients because it increases their confidence in their ability to maintain heal­thy habits. Another strategy to improve healthy eating is to make half your plate fruits and veggies every time you eat. This will automatically increase your intake of fiber, vitamins, minerals, antioxidants and other phytochemicals that help protect you from chronic disease and several types of cancer. Over time, you'll feel better, lighter, and your body will thank you!

Stay on top of your game. Don't wait until you feel chest pain, or you realize that you’re thirsty all the time, and urinating frequently (signs of diabetes). Schedule regular checkups. Certain diseases and conditions may be asymptomatic, so routine medical examinations are important for early detection before the problem gets out of hand.

Don’t wish it away. Pay attention to what your body is telling you, especially chest pain, shortness of breath, excessive thirst, and problems with urination. Have questions about your health or side effects due to a medication? Don’t self-diagnose by asking Dr. Google. Speak to your doctor or pharmacist. Let them be the experts. 

I wish all our Love Express men readers much health and blessings!
