Should You Eat Organic?

By Elizabeth Madison

It seems almost every day a client asks me questions about organic foods: should I only eat organic foods? Is non-organic food dangerous? What if I can’t afford organic food, should I just not buy any fruits and vegetables? There is a lot of confusing information in the media about organic foods, and at times, a lot of it is just hype. So to help my clients make informed buying decisions, I created a Q&A Organic Foods Fact Sheet. This month, I’m sharing some of these facts with you. I hope they empower you to choose wisely, and possibly save some money in the process.

What does ‘organic’ really mean? Organic is a term used to indicate foods or other agricultural products that have been produced through approved methods that incorporate cultural, biological, and mechanical practices that promote cycling of resources, ecological balance and biodiversity conservation. It does not allow the use of synthetic fertilizers, sew­age sludge, irradiation, and genetic engineering. Simply, organic farming is a commitment to sustainable farming methods that do not harm the environment.

How do you know a product is organic? This is where label reading is really important! Products that are made completely from orga­nic ingredients can be labeled as ‘100% organic’. Products that are made from 95% organic ingredients can be labeled as ‘organic’. Products made with at least 70% organic ingredients can be labeled as ‘made with organic ingredients.’ These are critical distinctions, as a lot of products tout their inclusion of organic ingredients, but it is the non-organic ingredients that may be a cause for concern.

Is organic the same as natural? Although used interchangeably, the terms ‘organic’ and ‘natural’ are not synonymous. Only foods and products that are grown and processed according to Uni­ted States Department of Agriculture (USDA) standards can be labeled organic. To ensure what you are buying is organic, look for the USDA organic label.

Is organic healthier than conventionally grown foods? In terms of nutrition content, there is no significant difference. This is a critical point if purchasing organic foods would create a strain on your budget. In this case, the priority should be ensuring that you and your family are eating sufficient fruits and vegetables daily. Now, as it relates to the amount of synthetic ingredients and chemicals used in growing food products, organic foods are superior to conventionally grown foods. They have been shown to have less of an impact on our environment and contain fewer additives, hormones and pesticides. This doesn’t mean you should avoid all non-organic foods. Although chronic ingestion of large do­ses of these chemicals have been linked to greater risk of asthmatic and allergy-related disorders, the amount used in conventional foods is far below the danger threshold. However, for those individuals with allergies, or diagnosed sensitivity to certain chemicals, organic foods may be a safe option, and allow for greater food variety.

Is it necessary to eat ALL organic? Again, it depends on your personal beliefs, and your budget. If you need to choose, here are some guidelines: any produce that requires peeling does not need to be organic. However, there are some exceptions. To make it easy, here are the ‘Clean Fifteen’ – those foods you don’t have to buy organic: avocadoes, sweet corn, pineapples, cabbage, sweet peas frozen, onions, asparagus, mangos, papayas, kiwi, eggplant, grapefruit, cantaloupe, cauliflower and sweet potatoes.

If you are going to buy organic, splurge on those with permeable or edible skin, such as the ‘Dirty Do­zen’: apples, peaches, nectarines, strawberries, grapes, celery, spinach, sweet bell peppers, cucumbers, cherry tomatoes, snap peas, and white potatoes.

For a more comprehensive list on what to buy organic, visit the Environmental Working Group’s website at www.ewg.org.

How about produce washes? Can they help reduce my exposure to pesticides and other chemi- cals? As magical as these cleaning products may sound, studies show that these pro­ducts do not remove pesticides and other chemicals, as these are mostly inside the food. Rinsing your produce with tap water is just as good.

It is important to be informed about the foods you put in your body. Don’t believe everything you hear in the media. We have to be alert and discerning, as this is God’s will. Test everything that is said. Hold on to what is good (1 Thessalonians 5: 21).
