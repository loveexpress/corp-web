Nutrition & Autoimmune Diseases

By Elizabeth Madison

In the last few years, I’ve seen an increase in the number of my clients that have been diagnosed with autoimmune diseases such as lupus, fibromyalgia, and rheumatoid arthritis. These are chronic conditions that can severely impact one’s quality of life. My clients always ask if they can treat their condition through healthy eating instead of medication. Unfortunately, most people will need medication to slow the progression of the diseases, but nutrition can play a significant role in the management of the inflammation and the symptoms.

Lupus attacks any part of the body including the skin, joints, and/or organs inside the body. Symptoms include fatigue, joint pain, rash, and fever.  Fibromyalgia is a condition in which the body attacks muscles and soft tissues. Symptoms include chronic muscle pain, fatigue, sleep problems and painful tender points or trigger points. Rheumatoid arthritis occurs when the immune system produces antibodies that attach to the linings of joints, causing an attack by the immune system. This causes the common symptoms of inflam-  mation, swelling and pain of the joints.

For all three conditions, several nutrition interventions have been shown to alleviate inflammation and relieve symptoms. The elimination of gluten, a gluten-free diet, may work for some people because it reduces the intake of refined carbohydrates(white bread, pasta, muffins,etc.),which promotes inflammation. A vegetarian diet has been shown to reduce symptoms for some people due to the high intake of anti-inflammatory foods.

Antioxidant-rich foods (fruits and vegetables) protect against cell damage which can decrease inflammation. Aim to eat 5 servings of fruits and vegetables per day: a piece of fruit during breakfast, two servings of fruits and vegetables at lunch, and two at dinner.   

Omega-3 fatty acids are    essential fats that have    anti-inflammatory properties, which help to reduce inflammation,redness,and swelling. Aim to eat omega-3-rich foods at least 2-3 times a week. An omega-3 supplement can help you meet your daily needs. Just make sure it has at least 250mgs of EPA and DHA each.

Herbs and spices such as ginger, curry, cinnamon, oregano, sage, cloves and garlic are rich in anti-inflammatory properties and antioxidants. Add a dash instead of the usual salt, and not only will you have a delicious,anti-inflammatory meal, but will also help control your blood pressure from the lower sodium intake.
