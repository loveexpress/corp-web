Jesus at the Box Office

Movie Review: WOODLAWN

High school football, racial tensions in the 70s and the Deep South might not sound like the stuff feel-good movies are made of, but Woodlawn manages to pull it off in what could be a Christian film that appeals to viewers outside of the Christian faithful.

While Woodlawn is a sports drama, the film takes viewers on a journey not so much about football, but about faith. Specifically, faith in Jesus Christ.  In 1970 Alabama, Tony Nathan, (played by Caleb Castille) comes from a strong African-American family who find themselves still living in widely segregated Alabama. Nathan has a gift that should catapult him to high school football stardom, but his gift feels more like a weight when he has to integrate into a white high school where racial tensions prevail.  

While Woodlawn High School is clearly not ready for integration, Hank Erwin, (Sean Austin) who refers to himself as a “sports chaplain” convinces  coach Tandy Geralds (Nic Bishop) to let him speak with his hostile players. Hank rather quickly convinces the football players to “choose love” and to accept Jesus.  Soon after, the teammates start praying together and attending Bible Study.  The entire school catches on and ultimately many of the students and teachers have a religious conversion. Coach Tandy is one of the last to convert, but he does so in a dramatic scene where he visits Nathan’s all black church and gets baptized.  

Woodlawn could have turned into a coach/player story because the relationship between Tandy and Tony is quite a profound one, but the Erwin Brothers (October Baby) steer clear of such a narrow focus to drill home the film’s overall message that faith can help a nation overcome its uglier practices.

Nathan turns into a high school football legend who is recruited by legendary University of Alabama coach Paul “Bear” Bryant (Jon Voight), who deserved more screen time for his impressive performance. The coach is so determined to sign Tony that he shows up at Tony’s door with a suitcase and refuses to leave until Tony agrees to attend “Alabama.”

Nathan’s courage, newfound faith, and football ability inspire the entire of state of Alabama to re evaluate their hearts, something not even the best integration laws could do. As Hank says ”When you play for yourself, you can do great, but when you play for something higher, that is when extraordinary things can happen. Nathan embodied this statement.

The movie does have flaws. Some of the conversions happen very quickly and the film glosses over some of the ugliest incidents in 1970s Birmingham, but examining these  vicious elements (while historically accurate)  would certainly have put a damper on the feel good film.

LaVonne McIver James is the founder and executive director of the Churches Making Movies Christian Film Festival. Follow her on twitter @churchmadefilms
