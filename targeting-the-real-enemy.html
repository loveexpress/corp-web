Targeting the Real Enemy

A young child once asked me, “Pastor Eddie, since God says we are to love our enemies, does that mean we are supposed to love the devil?”

Good question! Of course we should hate the devil! But do we really? The condition of our world saddens us, and we are grieved to see the hurt and misery Sa­tan causes. But does that mean we really hate Satan?

I realized that I had not allowed myself to hate anyone or anything. After all, I thought, hatred is sin, isn’t it? Like the did pointed out, didn’t Jesus say in Mt. 5:44, “Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you”? Didn’t Paul write in Romans 12:20, “If your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink”?

However, just a few ver­ses earlier, Paul also says, “Hate what is evil” Romans 12:9). He goes on to say “Ne­ver be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor” (v.11).

Had Satan and his works ever enraged me? Worse yet, had Satan stolen some of my zeal and spiritual fervor? Where was my fierce, zealous, warring heart? Where was my righteous indignation? Was it easier for me to be angry with a person who mistreated me than to be incensed by all that Satan was doing that was counter to God’s kingdom?

It was these kinds of questions that led me to train in spiritual warfare.The Lord’s primary method in my training was to take me to the Psalms, where He gave me a new warfare prayer vocabulary so I could deal effectively with my true enemies: Satan and his demons.

Mincing no words, Da­vid asks God to scoff at His enemies, dash them to pie­ces, strike them on the jaw, and break their teeth (Psalms. 2:49, 9, 3:7). That’s just in the first few Psalms! This vocabulary is powerful in equipping us to battle against our spiritual enemies-spiritual beings for whom there is no redemption (Eph. 6:12).

On the other hand, this vocabulary is not to be used to pray against a person. We are to pray for people. Even for wicked people, we are to pray for God’s mercy and not His judgement (James. 2:13).

Sadly, I’ve heard foolish, misguided Christians pray for homosexuals to become infected with AIDS “so they will learn that they cannot disobey God.” I’ve heard others reverse curses and even multiply them back up­on someone they suspect of having cursed them. But Je­sus said, “Bless those who curse you” (Luke. 6:28).

So how does all of this work? Take one of the Psalms that speaks against enemies and pray it against the powers of darkness. For example, you could pray from Psalm 35 something like this: “Fight against those spiritual forces that fight against me, Lord. Brandish spear and javelin against those who pursue; me. Disgrace and put to shame those who seek my life; may those who plot; my ruin be turned back in dismay; drive them away; pursue them down a dark and slippery path; entangle them in their own net; may they fall into the pit they dug for me. May all who gloat over my distress be put to shame and confusion; may all who exalt themselves over me be clothed with shame and disgrace.”

Many of the Psalms are easily adapted for this purpose. Some especially powerful ones include Psalms 10, 55, 58, 679, 109, and 137. Try praying th;em on your own to increase your personal warfare Vocabula­ry.

Finally, remember that our human enemies are people who have been entrap­ped by the devil, who has ta­ken them captive to do his will (2 Tim. 2:26). So let’s not pray judgement on these pri­soners of war. Instead ask God for the heart of a warrior to hate Satan and despite his demons so that together with Jesus, we can destroy the works of the devil (John. 3:8).

By Eddie Smith, presi­dent of the U.S. Prayer Center, and an internationally known conference spea­ker. This article is adapted from his booklet, Ammo: Building a Spiritual Warfare Vocabulary, available through www.prayer bookstore.com.
